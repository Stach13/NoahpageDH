Comparison of the Silmarillion and the Lord of the Rings

Though the diction and style used in the Silmarillion and Unfinished Tales are different than the Lord of the Rings, they all tell stories of adventures. In the N-gram chart you can see that difference between how many times “the sons of feanor” is used versus all the rest.

In this method, I adjusted the ngram count to 3 instead of 4 with "the sons of feanor" statement. This displays twice the frequency using "the sons of", which could leads others to believe that there were either a lot more than one group of "the sons of" or there was another "the sons of" who's tale is told just as frequent as the "sons of feanor".

Both novels use the same sort of descriptive language style to explain specific details in the books. In this example, under the KWIC (or Keywords in Context using Antconc) I found a prepositional phrase "the edge of the" which was one of the top ten listed under n-gram size 4. The KWIC shows exactly how descriptive each sentence is written, pulling a list of various samples. On the left Context it seems that words "to, from, toward, near, etc" show inferences of location. While the Right Context displays the exact connection between all three categories, with all the words ending in nouns after the phrase was used.

In this KWIC example, taken from the Silmarillion, it appears that many groups of words describe people or legions of people more often in the Silmarillion than in the Lord of the Rings. With many of the the Left Context nouns having correlations with the Hit phrase itself. Left Context ends with mostly a prepostional phrase heading into the Hit. The Right Context starts with mostly verbs such as "hearken, was, are, established, arise, etc." as well as prounouns such as "they and that".
